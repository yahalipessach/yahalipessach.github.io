Who Gets the Interest on Stablecoins?

11 Jul 2026

Circle, the issuer of the world's second-largest stablecoin, USDC, just became a bank. It still cannot take deposits, make loans or pay interest. But the OCC did grant it a national trust charter, so it can now hold your dollars under federal supervision and eventually run USDC's reserves itself.

The news arrives ten days after Visa, BlackRock, Coinbase and more than 140 other firms announced Open USD, a stablecoin that shares the yield earned on its Treasury reserves with the companies distributing it, rather than customers.

There are now roughly $305 billion of stablecoins in circulation, with the Treasury bills backing them earning close to $12 billion a year. The GENIUS Act resolved who receives that money by forbidding issuers from paying interest to holders, so the industry has had to find other ways to compete. Tether, the largest stablecoin issuer, does not pay its holders at all.

Coinbase, which distributes a great deal of USDC, pays 3.5% to customers who leave their holdings on the platform and subscribe to its loyalty programme. But this isn't available in many countries, and there are countless other crypto platforms with different terms. It's hard to pin down exactly how much of the $12 billion reaches holders, but we know it's not much.

Stablecoins may well make dollars more accessible and easier to move for people without reliable currencies. But the dollars sitting behind them still earn interest, and that interest usually ends up with the institutions built around them rather than the people holding the tokens.

History rhymes. After the Great Depression, American banks were barred from paying interest on chequing accounts. Without price competition, they too had to find new ways to compete; in some cases, banks enticed new customers by offering them free appliances for their home.

At least back then, you got a toaster.
